# How to Calculate If Your Onboarding Software Is Actually Worth It

## A worksheet approach with real numbers, not vendor math

*Originally published at [usertourkit.com](https://usertourkit.com/blog/calculate-onboarding-software-roi)*

You know onboarding matters. Your CFO wants a number. And every vendor's ROI page conveniently shows a 10x return without showing the math.

Here's the actual math.

Forrester puts the average return on structured onboarding at 5:1. But that number is a sanity check, not a forecast. The actual ROI depends on your product, your price point, and how bad your current onboarding is. A SaaS app with $49/month ACV and 3% trial-to-paid conversion will see a wildly different return from a $500/month enterprise tool converting 15% of trials.

## The formula breaks into three buckets

**Activation lift.** If 1,000 users sign up monthly and you move activation from 30% to 40% (the median SaaS rate to a reasonable target), that's an extra $35,280/year at a $49/month price point.

**Churn reduction.** 70% of SaaS customers churn within 90 days due to onboarding failures. Strong onboarding cuts churn 20-50%. On $50K MRR with 5% monthly churn, a 25% reduction saves $75,000/year.

**Support savings.** In-app guidance reduces support queries by 40%. At 200 tickets/month and $25/ticket, that's $24,000/year.

Total for our example: **$134,280/year** in gains.

## The cost side is where it gets interesting

Building onboarding in-house costs $60,000-$71,000 in year one for a startup, with $25,000+/year ongoing maintenance. That's from Appcues and Userpilot's research, cross-verified. At enterprise scale, Atlassian spent $3.5M over three years on onboarding tooling.

SaaS tools (Appcues, Userpilot, Pendo) run $1,000-$9,000/year at startup scale. But they charge per monthly active user. A company growing from 1,000 to 50,000 MAU can see Appcues costs balloon from $299/month to $4,000+/month.

Open-source libraries are the third option. $0-$99 upfront, no MAU scaling, but they require developer time to implement.

## The metric everyone gets wrong

Most teams measure completion rate. Chameleon's own metrics team calls this out as "a lagging input metric, not a revenue metric."

The right metric is cohort analysis: compare users who completed onboarding against those who didn't, then measure the revenue difference over 30, 60, and 90 days. That's the number that moves budget decisions.

A 3-step tour completes at 72%. A 7-step tour? Just 16%. The activation improvement comes from getting the right users through the critical path, not from getting everyone to finish every step.

## Four mistakes that inflate ROI calculations

1. **Ignoring implementation time.** SaaS tools take 1-4 weeks. Building in-house takes 1-3 months.
2. **Using vendor benchmarks as your baseline.** Vendor case studies show their best customers, not average outcomes.
3. **Modeling linear churn reduction.** Churn reduction compounds. Model over 12 months minimum.
4. **Forgetting the cost of switching.** Migration from one tool to another costs real engineering time.
