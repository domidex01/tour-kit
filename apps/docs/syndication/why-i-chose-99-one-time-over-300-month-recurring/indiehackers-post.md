## Title: Why I chose $99 one-time over $300/month recurring for my React library

**Body:**

I've been building Tour Kit, a headless product tour library for React. 10 packages, headless architecture, MIT core.

When it came to pricing Pro, I spent three weeks on a standard SaaS pricing page before scrapping it completely. Here's what happened.

**The realization:** Tour Kit is a library, not a SaaS. It ships in the buyer's bundle, runs locally, has no server. My marginal cost per additional user is effectively zero. Charging $79/month for something with no ongoing infrastructure cost felt dishonest.

**The data that confirmed it:**
- 78% of finance decision-makers are scrutinizing recurring costs harder
- 62% of businesses are consolidating SaaS subscriptions
- Median dev tool: $32/user/month ($384/year)
- $99 one-time = ~3 months of typical tool spend

**The boundary that matters:** Three core packages are MIT-licensed, free, and functionally complete for building tours. Eight Pro packages add adjacent capabilities (surveys, scheduling, analytics) for $99 once. The free tier isn't crippled to upsell. That distinction is what keeps the community on your side.

**What I got wrong:**
- Should have priced at $149 and run $99 launch sales (easier to discount than to raise)
- Launched with unlimited activations per license (generous but potentially unsustainable)
- ~40% of lifetime deal products shut down within 3 years (AppSumo data) so the economics deserve scrutiny

**Payments:** Polar.sh (4% + $0.40/tx, no monthly fees, automated license keys, open-source itself)

**The tradeoff I accept:** No MRR. Starting from zero every month. But "pay once, use forever" resonates in a way that subscription #131 never will.
