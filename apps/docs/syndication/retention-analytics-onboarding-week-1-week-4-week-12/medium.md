# Retention Analytics for Onboarding: What Week 1, Week 4, and Week 12 Actually Tell You

*How to measure whether your onboarding tour changes user behavior — not just user clicks*

*Originally published at [usertourkit.com](https://usertourkit.com/blog/retention-analytics-onboarding-week-1-week-4-week-12)*

Your onboarding tour has a 72% completion rate. Feels good, right? But completion doesn't tell you whether those users still exist in your product three months later.

Retention analytics for onboarding measures what happens after the tour ends. Specifically, it tracks the percentage of users who return to your product at defined intervals: week 1 (habit signal), week 4 (workflow integration), and week 12 (long-term stickiness).

According to Mixpanel's 2025 Product Benchmarks report, the median SaaS product retains 35% of users at week 1 and just 15% at week 12. Products with structured onboarding flows retain 2.6x more users at week 4 than those without.

## Why these three windows?

**Week 1** measures initial habit formation. Lenny Rachitsky's analysis of 50+ B2B SaaS companies found that 40-60% of users who sign up never return after day one. Week 1 tells you whether onboarding created enough value to warrant a second visit.

**Week 4** captures workflow integration. According to Amplitude's 2024 Product Report, the week 4 retention rate is the single strongest predictor of 12-month LTV across their dataset of 40,000+ digital products.

**Week 12** measures genuine stickiness. Three months is long enough for any novelty effect to fade. This is the number your investors actually care about.

## The formula

It's simpler than most analytics vendors make it look:

> Retention(N) = (Users active in week N) / (Users who completed onboarding in cohort week) x 100

"Active" means at least one meaningful session. Not a page load, not a background tab.

## What the benchmarks show

Products with guided onboarding tours see roughly 2x the retention at every checkpoint compared to unguided products. But that's correlation, not causation. Products that invest in onboarding also tend to invest in the rest of the user experience.

The more useful comparison: split your own users into completers vs. skippers. The retention delta between those two groups is your onboarding's actual lift.
