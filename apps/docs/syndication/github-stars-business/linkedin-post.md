There are 4.5 million fake stars on GitHub. You can buy them for less than the cost of a coffee.

I've been building an open-source React library as a solo developer. After a year of tracking every metric I could measure, here's the uncomfortable truth about GitHub stars:

Stars are a discovery mechanism, not a revenue signal. They feed GitHub's search algorithm, which makes your library findable. That's their actual business value. The correlation with downloads is moderate. The correlation with revenue is almost nonexistent without a separate monetization strategy.

OpenSSL secures virtually every HTTPS connection on the internet. Its annual donations? About $2,000.

The projects that turned stars into real revenue all share one pattern: they treated open source as a distribution channel, not a business model. Postiz hit $14,200/month MRR as a solo developer by charging for cloud hosting while keeping the code free.

The metric I track now instead of stars: time to second interaction. Someone who stars today and opens an issue tomorrow is worth more than a hundred people who starred and disappeared.
