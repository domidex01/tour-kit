## Title: GitHub Stars Don't Pay the Bills (But They Help)

## URL: https://usertourkit.com/blog/github-stars-business

## Comment to post immediately after:

I built Tour Kit, a headless React library for product tours, as a solo developer. This post is about what I learned tracking the relationship between GitHub stars and actual business outcomes over the past year.

The tl;dr: stars are a discovery mechanism (they feed GitHub's search ranking algorithm), not a revenue signal. The correlation between stars and npm downloads is moderate for JavaScript libraries and weak for everything else. And the gap between "someone starred my repo" and "someone paid for the premium tier" is enormous.

Some numbers that surprised me during research: 4.5 million suspected fake stars detected in a 2024 study, fake stars available for EUR 0.08 each, OpenSSL receiving ~$2,000/year in donations despite securing the entire internet. On the other end, Postiz reached $14,200/month MRR as a solo dev by using open source for awareness and charging for cloud hosting.

The main lesson I took away: open source is a distribution model, not a business model. Stars help with the first half of the equation (making something people find), but not the second half (convincing them to pay).

Happy to answer questions about the specific metrics I track now instead of stars, or the growth patterns I observed.
