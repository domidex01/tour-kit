# What Is Feature Adoption? A Developer's Guide to Measuring What Matters

### Most teams track launches. The ones that grow track repeat usage.

*Originally published at [usertourkit.com](https://usertourkit.com/blog/what-is-feature-adoption)*

Most SaaS teams ship features and hope for the best. A month later, 8% of users touched the new thing they spent a quarter building. Feature adoption is the discipline of closing that gap.

## What feature adoption actually means

Feature adoption is the process by which users discover, try, and repeatedly use a specific capability within your product. A feature is "adopted" when a user returns to it unprompted because it solves a real problem.

As of April 2026, industry benchmarks place healthy adoption rates between 20-30% for general features, with core workflow features targeting 60-80%.

Don't confuse it with product adoption. Product adoption asks "is this person using our app?" Feature adoption asks "is this person using *this specific part* of our app?"

## The four stages

Feature adoption follows a funnel: **Exposed** (user discovers it), **Activated** (user tries it), **Used** (meaningful engagement), and **Used again** (returns without prompting).

The first two stages are where onboarding tools have the most impact. You can't adopt what you don't know exists.

As Adrian Raudaschl writes in Smashing Magazine's TARS framework: "Sometimes, low feature adoption has nothing to do with the feature itself, but rather where it sits in the UI."

## The formula

Feature Adoption Rate = (Feature MAUs / Total Logins) x 100

If 1,200 users interact with your dashboard filter in a month out of 5,000 total logins, your adoption rate is 24%.

## What "good" looks like

- **Core workflow features** (dashboard, editor, search): 60-80%
- **Secondary features** (filters, export, sharing): 30-50%
- **Specialized features** (API access, integrations): 15-30%
- **Power-user features** (keyboard shortcuts, bulk ops): 5-15%

Real examples: Dropbox Paper hit 20% adoption in 6 months. Spotify Blend reached 30% among premium users. Both are considered successful.

Customers who adopt new features regularly are 31% less likely to churn.

## Three strategies that actually work

**Contextual nudges over blast announcements.** A tooltip that appears when a user is doing the exact task the new feature improves gets clicked. A banner saying "New feature!" gets dismissed.

**Set thresholds per feature.** "Used once" isn't adopted. Define what repeated use means based on expected frequency.

**Connect adoption to business outcomes.** Track whether users who adopt feature X retain better or submit fewer support tickets.
