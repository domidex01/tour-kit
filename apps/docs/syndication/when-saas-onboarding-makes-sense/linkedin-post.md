I build an open-source product tour library. My business depends on developers choosing code over SaaS platforms.

So when I say SaaS onboarding tools are the right choice for many teams, I mean it.

After watching teams adopt both approaches, the pattern is clear: the decision isn't about technology. It's about who owns onboarding at your company.

If a product manager iterates on flows weekly, a code library creates a developer bottleneck. SaaS visual editors eliminate it entirely.

If engineering owns onboarding as product code, a library gives you performance control (8KB vs third-party scripts), design system consistency, and cost predictability at scale.

Some numbers:
- 35% of enterprises have replaced SaaS with custom builds (Retool 2026)
- SaaS onboarding costs $89-$595/mo, scaling with MAU
- At 100K+ MAU, annual SaaS costs can exceed $50K

The honest answer: both approaches have room. The question is which trade-offs your team can live with.
