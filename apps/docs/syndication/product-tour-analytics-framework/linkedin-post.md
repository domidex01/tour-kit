Most teams measure product tour success with a single number: completion rate.

The average across 15 million interactions is 61%. That sounds reasonable. But here's the problem: completion rate tells you nothing about whether the tour actually changed user behavior.

I built a two-layer measurement framework that connects tour-level metrics (completion, step drop-off, trigger quality) to product-level outcomes (activation rate, feature adoption, retention).

Some findings from the benchmark data:
- Each tour step past three costs 15-20 percentage points in completion
- Self-serve tours outperform auto-triggered ones by 123%
- Teams that pair tour analytics with product analytics can actually attribute behavior changes to specific tours

The key insight for engineering leaders: if your team can't answer "did this tour improve activation?", your analytics setup has a gap. A 10% holdout group and a two-layer dashboard fixes it.
