# What Is Product Stickiness? The One Metric That Predicts Whether Users Stay

*The median SaaS product has a 9.3% stickiness rate. Here's what that means and how to fix it.*

*Originally published at [usertourkit.com](https://usertourkit.com/blog/what-is-product-stickiness)*

---

Most SaaS teams track signups and churn. Product stickiness is the metric that sits between those two numbers and tells you whether people actually come back.

## The definition

Product stickiness is the degree to which users return to a product repeatedly because they find it valuable. The standard measurement is the DAU/MAU ratio: daily active users divided by monthly active users, expressed as a percentage.

A product with 2,500 DAU and 10,000 MAU has a 25% stickiness rate. As of April 2026, the median SaaS stickiness rate is just 9.3%. Anything above 20% is good; 50% or higher is elite territory reserved for products like WhatsApp and Slack.

Sequoia Capital has long used DAU/MAU as a core investment metric, noting that the standard ratio is 10-20% for most companies.

## How it works

The formula is simple: `(DAU / MAU) × 100`

The hard part is defining "active." Page views don't count. An active user should be someone who performed a meaningful action that corresponds to your product's core value.

For B2B teams, there's a hidden trap: calendar-day DAU/MAU punishes products that aren't used on weekends. A project management tool with perfect weekday engagement maxes out around 71%. Gainsight recommends a workday-adjusted calculation, which pushes the real B2B benchmark from 13% to roughly 40%.

DAU/MAU is also a lagging indicator. As David Sacks points out, any movement won't be noticeable immediately because the MAU denominator smooths out short-term changes. For faster feedback, the "Lness distribution" — measuring how many days per week or month a user is active — gives more granular signal.

## Benchmarks by industry

The numbers vary wildly by category:

- Social media and messaging: 50-80%
- Productivity tools: 40-60%
- B2B SaaS (workday-adjusted): ~40%
- Gaming: 30-40%
- B2C consumer apps: 20-50%
- B2B SaaS (calendar-day): 10-20%
- E-commerce: ~9.8%

Comparing across categories is meaningless. A 15% rate is poor for messaging and excellent for e-commerce.

## What drives stickiness

Stickiness is a leading indicator of retention. Companies with the highest net revenue retention posted median growth 83% higher than the population median.

Three patterns matter most:

**Speed to activation.** Users who activate within three days are 90% more likely to keep using a product. WeMoney saw a 20% retention boost from integrating goal-setting into onboarding.

**Habit loops.** Products that embed into daily workflows build patterns that are hard to break. The Twilio report found 57% of consumers spend more with brands that personalize their experience.

**Feedback timing.** Collecting feedback at the right moments — after milestones, not mid-task — identifies friction before it becomes churn.
